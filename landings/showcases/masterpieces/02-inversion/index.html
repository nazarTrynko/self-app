Perspective Transformation

THE INVERSION

Don't argue against your opposition.
Become them until they're not enemies anymore.

YOU THEM
"You don't understand your enemy until you can argue their position more convincingly than they can. Not as a trick—as genuine embodiment."

The Inversion Practice

Enter a belief you hold strongly. Then, become its opposite.

1

Steel Man

What is the strongest possible version of the opposing view? Not a strawman—the most intelligent, most charitable interpretation.

2

Understand

What experiences, values, or reasoning would lead someone to genuinely believe this? Not "they're stupid"—real understanding.

3

Embody

Now write a paragraph AS IF you believed this position. Not mocking—genuine advocacy. Make it convincing.

4

Integrate

What did you learn? What grain of truth exists in the opposing view that your original position might be missing?

Common Inversions

"Hard work leads to success" ↔ "Success is largely luck and circumstance"

What if your success was mostly due to factors outside your control? What changes?

"People are fundamentally good" ↔ "People are fundamentally self-interested"

How would you live differently if everyone was primarily looking out for themselves?

"Technology will save us" ↔ "Technology will be our downfall"

What legitimate concerns exist about our technological trajectory?

"I know who I am" ↔ "The self is an illusion"

What if there is no fixed "you"—just a stream of experiences claiming continuity?

Beyond Debate

Debate is about winning. Inversion is about understanding. When you can genuinely inhabit the opposing view, you transcend the conflict. There's no enemy—only different vantage points.

The Inversion

Every other tool helps you strengthen your position. This one asks you to genuinely abandon it—temporarily. The goal isn't to change your mind. It's to understand how you could have a different mind.

The Belief Turing Test

Could you argue the opposing position so well that someone who holds it would think you believe it? If not, you don't yet understand it. Keep practicing.

Think of someone you disagree with strongly. Not their worst argument—their best one. Not their motivation as you imagine it—as they experience it. Now ask: what would it take for you to believe what they believe? The answer reveals something about both of you.
